Reinventing Paper

Imagine if you could fit a computer keyboard, piano, and video game controller in the fold of your pocket. Using paper electronics, a seemingly ordinary piece of paper can be transformed into a piece of powerful technology. Users are able to input information such as letters, numbers, action buttons, and music notes using paper keys which are activated by the friction of the user’s fingers tapping on the paper. The friction between the user’s fingers creates electric static that is carried through a circuit on the back of the paper which attaches to a bluetooth microchip. This bluetooth microchip is the brain of the paper keyboard and acts as the control unit, ALU, and memory. The microchip’s ALU calculates and processes the electric static emitted by the user as they are tapping. The pattern and order in which the electric signals occur is stored on the microchip’s memory and then acts as a control unit by sending a signal to a computer which executes the instructions. The signal’s output is text, sounds from music notes, and movement on the computer screen from the action buttons on the paper keyboard.
